All the 66 Books in the Bible

The length of the Bible—about 611,000 words spread across 66 shorter texts known as its "books"—is enormous. This is so because the Bible is a compilation of texts from several authors who lived in various eras. We can read the Bible in "chunks" rather than having to read it all at once, which in some ways makes it simpler to approach. However, it also adds some confusion. The Bible is a book unto itself. The Latin and Greek words for "book" (biblia and biblos, respectively) are actually where the word "bible" originates. It's a collection of books, though. Here is a summary of each Bible book.



The Old Testament



The Old Testament includes 39 books which were written long before Jesus was born. The first five books of the Bible are called the Torah, or the Law of Moses.

THE LAW

Genesis: Chapter 1-50

1. Genesis answers two of massive questions: “How did God’s relationship with the world begin?” and “Where did the nation of Israel come from?” This is where the stories of Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, Noah and the Ark, the Tower of Babel, Abraham and Isaac, Jacob’s ladder, and Joseph’s coat of many colors are recorded. Author: Traditionally Moses, but the stories are much older. Genesis means origin orthe begginings. Ranging from the cosmos to the family, this wide-sweping book tells of the universe, the origin of the human race, the beginning of sin and sufferig in the world, and about God's way of dealing with humanity. Genesis can be divided into two main parts: 1st. The creation of the world and the early history of the human race. Here are accounts af Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, Noah and the flood, and together with the Tower of Babel (Chapters 1-11). 2nd. The History of the early ancestors of the Israelites.The first is Abraham, who was notable for his faith and his obedience to God. Thenfollow the stories of his son Isaac, and grandson Jacob (also known as Israel), and of Jacob's twelve sons, who were the founders of the twelve tribes of Israel. Particular attension is given to one of the sons, Joseph, and the events that brought Jacob and his other sons with their families to live in Egypt(Chapters 12-50). While the Book of Genesis tells stories about people, it is first and foremost an account of what God has done. Introducing the drama of redemption, it begins with the affirmation that God created the universe, and it ends with a promise that God will continue to show his concern for his people. Throughout the book the main character is God, who creates human beings to live in his world, leads and helps his people, judges and punishes those who do wrong, and shapes their history. This ancient book was written to record the story of a people's faith and to help keep that faith alive. Outline of Contents Creation of the universe and of the human race 1:1-2:25 The beginning of sin and suffering 3:1-24 From Adam to Noah 4:1-5:32 Noah and the flood 6:1-10:32 The Tower of Babel 11:1-9 From Shem to Abram 11:10-32 The patriarchs: Abraham, Isaac, jacob 12:1-35:29 The descendants of Esau 36:1-45:28 The Israelites in Egypt 46:1-50:26

2. Exodus: Chapter 1-40

God saved Israel from slavery in Egypt, and then ventures into a special relationship with them. Author: Traditionally Moses Exodus means depature, and refers to the most important event in Israel's history, which is described in this book - The freeing of te Israelites from bondage and their depature from Egypt, where they had been enslaved by the Pharoah. The Book of Exodus has four main parts: 1st The miraculous freeing of the Hebrews from slavery; 2nd Their dramatic journey to Mount Sinai; 3rd God's covenant with his people at Snai, which gave them moral, civil, and religious laws to live by; 4th The building and furnishing of a place of worship for Israel, and laws regarding the priests and the worship of God. Above all, Exodus describes what God did, liberating his enslaved people and forming them into a nation with hope for the future. The lord is shown to be the one true God, ruler of the universe, whose will is unstopaable. The central human figure in the book is Moses, the man whom God chose to lead his people from Egypt. The most widely known part of the book is the list of the 'Ten Commandments' (20:1-17). Outline of Contents The Israelites set free from Egypt 1:1-15:21 a, Slaves in Egypt 1:1-22 b, Moses' birth and early life 2:1-4:31 c, Moses and Aaron confront the king of Egypt 12:1-15:21 From the Red Sea to Mount Snai 15:22-18:27 The law and the covenant 19:1-24:18 The Sacred Tent of Instructions for worship 25:1-40:38

3.Leviticus: Chapter 1-27

God gives Israelites instructions and guide for how to worship and praise Him. Author: traditionally Moses Leviticus is named after the tribe of Levi, from which came all the priests and all those who looked after the religious ceremonies associated with the Tent of Meeting and later the Temple. The Book of Leviticus contains the regulations for worship and religious ceremonies in encient Israel, and for the priests who were carrying out these instructions. The main theme of the book is the holiness of God and the ways in which his people were to worship and live so as to maintain their relationship with him. The best known words from the book are those which Jesus called the second great commandment: "love your neighbour as yourself" (19:18) Outline of Contents Laws about offerings and sacrifices 1:1-7:38 The ordination of Aaron and his sons as priests 8:1-10:20 Laws about ritual cleanness and uncleanness 11:1-15:33 The Day of Atonement 16:1-34 Laws about holiness in life and worship 17:1-27:34

4. Numbers: Chapter 1-36

Israel is failing to trust and obey God, and wanders in the wilderness for a period of 40 years. Author: Traditionally Moses The book of Numbers is the account of Israelites' life in the desert from the time they left Mount Sinai until they reached the eastern border of Canaan, the land that God had promised to give them, a journey which took them nearly forty years. Numbers, the name of the book, emphisises an important feature of the story, the two censuses of the Israelites which Moses orginised, first at Mount Sinai before their depature, and again in Moab, east of the Jordan, about a generation later. In the period between Moses' two censuses, the Israelites went to Kadesh Brnea of the southern border of Canaan, but they failed to rnter the promised land from there. They spent many years in that area before proceeding to the region east of the River Jordan, where some of the people settled while the others prepared to cross the river into Canaan, their promised land. Numbers tells of people who were often discouraged and afraid in the face of hardship, people who rebelled against God and against Moses, the man God appointed to lead them. Yet it is also the story of God's faithfulness, his persistent care for his people in spite of their weakness and disobedience. It is also the record of Moses, steadfast, if sometimes impatient, devotion of both to God and his people. Outline of Contents The Israelites prepare to leave Mount Sinai 1:1-9:23 a, The first census 1:1-4:49 b, Various laws and rules 5:1-8:26 c, The second Passover 9:1-23 From Mount Sinai to Moab 10:1-21:35 Events in Moab 22:1-32:42 Summay of the journey from Egypt to Moab 33:1-49 Instructions before crossing the jordan 33:50-36:13

5. Deuteronomy: Chapter 1-34

Moses is giving Israel instructions and guide (in a way, it is a recap of the laws in Exodus–Numbers) for how to love and obey God in the Promised Land. Author: Traditionally Moses The framework of the book of Deuteronomy ('second law') is a series of addresses given by Moses to the people of Israel in Moab, where they had stopped at the end of their long desert journey and were about to enter Canaan, the land God has promised them. Some of the most important matters recorded in Deuteronomy are: 1st Moses' retelling of the great events of the past forty years in the desert, appearing to the people to remember how God led them through the their long journey and to be loyal and obedient to God. 2nd MMoses review the law given to him on Mount Sinai, the 'The Ten Commandments', emphrasing the meaning of the first laws which are to govern Israel's life in their promised land. 3rd Moses' powerful reminder to the people of the serious meaning of God's covanant with them. Moses calls upon them to renew their commitment to it and to their obligation to God. 4th Joshua's commissioning as the next leader upon the tribes of Israel, Moses in Moab, east of the Jordan River. The great theme of Deuteronomy is that God has saved and blessed his chosen people whom he loves. His people must remember this and obey and love God so that they may have life and continued blessing. The key verses of this book contain the words that Jesus called the greatest of all commandments: "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength" (6:5) Outline of Contents Moses' first address 1:1-4:49 Moses' second address 5:1-26:19 a, The 'Ten Commadments' 5:1-10:22 b, Laws, rules and warnings 11:1-26:19 Instructions about entering Canaan 27:1-28:68 Renewing the covenant 29:1-30:20 Moses' last words 31:1-33:29 The death of Moses 34:1-12

HISTORY

6. Joshua: Chapter 1-24

Joshua becomes Israel’s new leader and leads Israel to conquer the Promised land, then parcels out territories to the twelve tribes of Israel. The Battle of Jericho and the day the sun stood still, but most of the action happens in the first half of this book. The last half is mostly about divvying in to real estate. Author: Nobody knows The book of Joshua is the story of the Israelite invasion of Canaan, the land God has promised them, under the leadership of Joshua, the successor of Moses, it also sets out the division of Canaan among the tribes. Notable events recorded in this book include the crossing of the Jordan, the fall of Jericho, the battle at Ai, and the renewal of the covenant between God and his people. One of the best knownpassages in the book is, "choose for your selves this day whom you will serve...as for me and my household, we will serve the LORD" (24:15) Outline of Contents The conquest of Canaan 1:1-12:24 The division of the land 13:1-21:45 a, Theland east of the Jordan 12:1-33 b, The land west of the Jordan 14:1-19:51 c, The cities of refuge 20:1-45 d, The cities of the Levites 21:1-34 The eastern tribes return to their territory 22:1-34 Joshua's farewell address 23:1-16 The covenant renewed at Shechem 24:1-33

7. Judges: Chapter 1-21

Israel enters a cycle of turning from God, falling captive to oppressive nations, calling out to God, and being rescued by leaders God sends their way (called “judges”). Author: Nobody knows The book of Judges contains accounts of events which took place during the lawless period of Israel's history between the invasion of Canaan and the establishment of the monarchy. These stories are about the exploits of national heroes called 'judges', most of whom were military leaders rather than judges in the strictly legal sense of the word. One of the better known of them was Samson, whose deeds are recorded in chapters 13-16. The great and obvious lesson of Judges is that Israel's very survival depended on loyalty to God, while disloyalty always led to disaster. But at another level there is a deeper, underlying truth: even when the nation was disloyal to God, and even when disaster inevitably came, God was always ready to save his people when they repented and turned to him again. Outline of Contents Events up to the death of Joshua 1:1-2:10 The judges of Israel 2:11-16:31 Various incidents 17:1-21:25

8. Ruth: Chapter 1-4

Two widows lose everything, and find hope in Israel—which leads to the birth of the future King David. Author: Nobody knows

9. 1 Samuel: Chapter 1-31

Israel demands a king, who turns out to be quite a disappointment. Author: Nobody knows

10. 2 Samuel: Chapter 1-24

David, a man after God’s own heart, becomes king of Israel. Author: Nobody knows

11. 1 Kings: Chapter 1-22

The kingdom of Israel has a time of peace and prosperity under King Solomon, but afterward splits, and the two lines of kings turn away from God. Author: Nobody knows

12. 2 Kings: Chapter 1-25

Both kingdoms ignore God and his prophets, until they both fall captive to other world empires. Author: Nobody knows

13. 1 Chronicles: Chapter 1-29

This is a brief history of Israel from Adam to David, culminating with David commissioning the temple of God in Jerusalem. Author: Traditionally Ezra

14. 2 Chronicles: Chapter 1-36

David’s son Solomon builds the temple, but after centuries of rejecting God, the Babylonians take the southern Israelites captive and destroy the temple. Author: Traditionally Ezra

15. Ezra: Chapter 1-10

The Israelites rebuild the temple in Jerusalem, and a scribe named Ezra teaches the people to once again obey God’s laws. Author: Ezra

16. Nehemiah: Chapter 1-13

The city of Jerusalem is in bad shape, so Nehemiah rebuilds the wall around the city. Author: Nehemiah

17. Esther: Chapter 1-10

Someone hatches a genocidal plot to bring about Israel’s extinction, and Esther must face the emperor to ask for help. Author: Nobody knows

POETRY & WISDOM

18. Job: Chapter 1-42

Satan attacks a righteous man named Job, and Job and his friends argue about why terrible things are happening to him. Author: Nobody knows

19. Psalms: Chapter 1-150

A collection of 150 songs that Israel sang to God (and to each other)—kind of like a hymnal for the ancient Israelites. Author: So many authors including David.

20. Proverbs: Chapter 1-31

A collection of sayings written to help people make wise decisions that bring about justice. Author: Solomon and other wise men

21. Ecclesiates: Chapter 1-12

A philosophical exploration of the meaning of life—with a surprisingly nihilistic tone for the Bible. Author: Traditionally Solomon

22. Song of Solomon: Chapter 1-8

A love song (or collection of love songs) celebrating love, desire, and marriage. Author: Traditionally Solomon (but it could have been written about Solomon, or in the style of Solomon)

MAJOR PROPHETS

23. Isaiah: Chapter 1-66

God sends the prophet Isaiah to warn Israel of future judgment—but also to tell them about a coming king and servant who will “bear the sins of many.” Author: Isaiah (and maybe some of his followers)

24. Jeremiah: Chapter 1-52

God sends a prophet to warn Israel about the coming Babylonian captivity, but the people don’t take the news very well. Author: Jeremiah

25. Lamentations: Chapter 1-5

A collection of dirges lamenting the fall of Jerusalem after the Babylonian attacks. Author: Traditionally Jeremiah

26. Ezekiel: Chapter 1-48

God chooses a man to speak for Him to Israel, to tell them the error of their ways and teach them justice: Ezekiel. Author: Ezekiel

27. Daniel: Chapter 1-12

Daniel becomes a high-ranking wise man in the Babylonian and Persian empires, and has prophetic visions concerning Israel’s future. Author: Daniel (with other contributors)

MINOR PROPHETS

28. Hosea: Chapter 1-14

Hosea is told to marry a prostitute who leaves him, and he must bring her back: a picture of God’s relationship with Israel. Author: Hosea

29. Joel: Chapter 1-3

God sends a plague of locusts to Judge Israel, but his judgment on the surrounding nations is coming, too. Author: Joel

30. Amos: Chapter 1-9

A shepherd named Amos preaches against the injustice of the Northern Kingdom of Israel. Author: Amos

31. Obadiah: Chapter 1

Obadiah warns the neighboring nation of Edom that they will be judged for plundering Jerusalem. Author: Obadiah

32. Jonah: Chapter 1-4

A disobedient prophet runs from God, is swallowed by a great fish, and then preaches God’s message to the city of Nineveh. Author: Traditionally Jonah

33. Micah: Chapter 1-7

Micah confronts the leaders of Israel and Judah regarding their injustice, and prophecies that one day the Lord himself will rule in perfect justice. Author: Micah

34. Nahum: Chapter 1-3

Nahum foretells of God’s judgment on Nineveh, the capital of Assyria. Author: Nahum

35. Habakkuk: Chapter 1-3

Habakkuk pleads with God to stop the injustice and violence in Judah, but is surprised to find that God will use the even more violent Babylonians to do so. Author: Habakkuk

36. Zephaniah: Chapter 1-3

God warns that he will judge Israel and the surrounding nations, but also that he will restore them in peace and justice. Author: Zephaniah

37. Haggai: Chapter 1-2

The people have abandoned the work of restoring God’s temple in Jerusalem, and so Haggai takes them to task. Author: Haggai

38. Zechariah: Chapter 1-14

The prophet Zechariah calls Israel to return to God, and records prophetic visions that show what’s happening behind the scenes.

39. Malachi: Chapter 1-4

God has been faithful to Israel, but they continue to live disconnected from him—so God sends Malachi to call them out.

New Testament

THE GOSPELS

The New Testament includes 27 books about Jesus’ ministry and what it means to follow him. The first four books of the New Testament are called the Gospels.

40. Mathew: Chapter 1-28

This is an account of Jesus’ life, death, and resurrection, focusing on Jesus’ role as the true king of the Jews. Author: Matthew

41. Mark: Chapter 1-16

This brief account of Jesus’ earthly ministry highlights Jesus’ authority and servanthood. Author: John Mark

42. Luke: Chapter 1-24

Luke writes the most thorough account of Jesus’ life, pulling together eyewitness testimonies to tell the full story of Jesus. Author: Luke

43. John: Chapter 1-21

John lists stories of signs and miracles with the hope that readers will believe in Jesus. Author: John

HISTORY

44.Acts: Chapter 1-28

Jesus returns to the Father, the Holy Spirit comes to the church, and the gospel of Jesus spreads throughout the world. Author: Luke

POUL'S LETTERS

45. Romans: Chapter 1-16

Paul summarizes how the gospel of Jesus works in a letter to the churches at Rome, where he plans to visit. Author: Paul

46. 1 Corinthians: Chapter 1-16

Paul writes a disciplinary letter to a fractured church in Corinth, and answers some questions that they’ve had about how Christians should behave. Author: Paul

47. 2 Corinthians: Chapter 1-13

Paul writes a letter of reconciliation to the church at Corinth, and clears up some concerns that they have. Author: Paul

48. Galatians: Chapter 1-6

Paul hears that the Galatian churches have been lead to think that salvation comes from the law of Moses, and writes a (rather heated) letter telling them where the false teachers have it wrong. Author: Paul

49. Ephesians: Chapter 1-6

Paul writes to the church at Ephesus about how to walk in grace, peace, and love. Author: Paul

50. Philippians: Chapter 1-4

An encouraging letter to the church of Philippi from Paul, telling them how to have joy in Christ.

51. Colossians: Chapter 1-4

Paul writes the church at Colossae a letter about who they are in Christ, and how to walk in Christ. Author: Paul

52. 1 Thessalonians: Chapter 1-5

Paul has heard a good report on the church at Thessalonica, and encourages them to “excel still more” in faith, hope, and love. Author: Paul

53. 2 Thessalonians: Chapter 1-3

Paul instructs the Thessalonians on how to stand firm until the coming of Jesus. Author: Paul

54. 1 Timothy: Chapter 1-6

Paul gives his protegé Timothy instruction on how to lead a church with sound teaching and a godly example. Author: Paul

55. 2 Timothy: Chapter 1-4

Paul is nearing the end of his life, and encourages Timothy to continue preaching the word. Author: Paul

56. Titus: Chapter 1-3

Paul advises Titus on how to lead orderly, counter-cultural churches on the island of Crete. Author: Paul

57. Philemon: Chapter 1

Paul strongly recommends that Philemon accept his runaway slave as a brother, not a slave. Author: Paul

GENERAL LETTERS

58. Hebrews: Chapter 1-13

A letter encouraging Christians to cling to Christ despite persecution, because he is greater. Author: Nobody knows

59. James: Chapter 1-5

A letter telling Christians to live in ways that demonstrate their faith in action. Author: James (likely the brother of Jesus)

60. 1 Peter: Chapter 1-5

Peter writes to Christians who are being persecuted, encouraging them to testify to the truth and live accordingly. Author: Peter

61. 2 Peter: Chapter 1-3

Peter writes a letter reminding Christians about the truth of Jesus, and warning them that false teachers will come. Author: Peter

62. 1 John: Chapter 1-5

John writes a letter to Christians about keeping Jesus’ commands, loving one another, and important things they should know. Author: John

63. 2 John: Chapter 1

A very brief letter about walking in truth, love, and obedience. Author: John

64. 3 John: Chapter 1

An even shorter letter about Christian fellowship. Author: John

65. Jude: Chapter 1

A letter encouraging Christians to contend for the faith, even though ungodly persons have crept in unnoticed. Author: Jude

PROPHECY

66. Revelation: Chapter 1-22

John sees visions of things that have been, things that are, and things that are yet to come. Author: John
