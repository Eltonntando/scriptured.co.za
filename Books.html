All the 66 Books in the Bible

The length of the Bible is about 611,000 words spread across 66 shorter texts known as its "books"—is enormous. This is so because the Bible is a compilation of texts from several authors who lived in various eras. We can read the Bible in "chunks" rather than having to read it all at once, which in some ways makes it simpler to approach. However, it also adds some confusion. The Bible is a book unto itself. The Latin and Greek words for "book" (biblia and biblos, respectively) are actually where the word "bible" originates. It's a collection of books, though. Here is a summary of each Bible book.



The Old Testament



The Old Testament includes 39 books which were written long before Jesus was born. The first five books of the Bible are called the Torah, or the Law of Moses.

THE LAW

Genesis: Chapter 1-50

1. Genesis answers two of massive questions: “How did God’s relationship with the world begin?” and “Where did the nation of Israel come from?” This is where the stories of Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, Noah and the Ark, the Tower of Babel, Abraham and Isaac, Jacob’s ladder, and Joseph’s coat of many colors are recorded. Author: Traditionally Moses, but the stories are much older. Genesis means origin orthe begginings. Ranging from the cosmos to the family, this wide-sweping book tells of the universe, the origin of the human race, the beginning of sin and sufferig in the world, and about God's way of dealing with humanity. Genesis can be divided into two main parts: 1st. The creation of the world and the early history of the human race. Here are accounts af Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, Noah and the flood, and together with the Tower of Babel (Chapters 1-11). 2nd. The History of the early ancestors of the Israelites.The first is Abraham, who was notable for his faith and his obedience to God. Thenfollow the stories of his son Isaac, and grandson Jacob (also known as Israel), and of Jacob's twelve sons, who were the founders of the twelve tribes of Israel. Particular attension is given to one of the sons, Joseph, and the events that brought Jacob and his other sons with their families to live in Egypt(Chapters 12-50). While the Book of Genesis tells stories about people, it is first and foremost an account of what God has done. Introducing the drama of redemption, it begins with the affirmation that God created the universe, and it ends with a promise that God will continue to show his concern for his people. Throughout the book the main character is God, who creates human beings to live in his world, leads and helps his people, judges and punishes those who do wrong, and shapes their history. This ancient book was written to record the story of a people's faith and to help keep that faith alive. God saved Israel from slavery in Egypt, and then ventures into a special relationship with them. The Book of Exodus has four main parts: 1st The miraculous freeing of the Hebrews from slavery; 2nd Their dramatic journey to Mount Sinai; 3rd God's covenant with his people at Snai, which gave them moral, civil, and religious laws to live by; 4th The building and furnishing of a place of worship for Israel, and laws regarding the priests and the worship of God. Above all, Exodus describes what God did, liberating his enslaved people and forming them into a nation with hope for the future. 4. Numbers: Chapter 1-36

Israel is failing to trust and obey God, and wanders in the wilderness for a period of 40 years. Author: Traditionally Moses The book of Numbers is the account of Israelites' life in the desert from the time they left Mount Sinai until they reached the eastern border of Canaan, the land that God had promised to give them, a journey which took them nearly forty years. Numbers, the name of the book, emphisises an important feature of the story, the two censuses of the Israelites which Moses orginised, first at Mount Sinai before their depature, and again in Moab, east of the Jordan, about a generation later. In the period between Moses' two censuses, the Israelites went to Kadesh Brnea of the southern border of Canaan, but they failed to rnter the promised land from there. They spent many years in that area before proceeding to the region east of the River Jordan, where some of the people settled while the others prepared to cross the river into Canaan, their promised land. Numbers tells of people who were often discouraged and afraid in the face of hardship, people who rebelled against God and against Moses, the man God appointed to lead them. Yet it is also the story of God's faithfulness, his persistent care for his people in spite of their weakness and disobedience. It is also the record of Moses, steadfast, if sometimes impatient, devotion of both to God and his people. Moses is giving Israel instructions and guide (in a way, it is a recap of the laws in Exodus–Numbers) for how to love and obey God in the Promised Land. 7. Judges: Chapter 1-21

Israel enters a cycle of turning from God, falling captive to oppressive nations, calling out to God, and being rescued by leaders God sends their way (called “judges”). Author: Nobody knows The book of Judges contains accounts of events which took place during the lawless period of Israel's history between the invasion of Canaan and the establishment of the monarchy. These stories are about the exploits of national heroes called 'judges', most of whom were military leaders rather than judges in the strictly legal sense of the word. One of the better known of them was Samson, whose deeds are recorded in chapters 13-16. The great and obvious lesson of Judges is that Israel's very survival depended on loyalty to God, while disloyalty always led to disaster. But at another level there is a deeper, underlying truth: even when the nation was disloyal to God, and even when disaster inevitably came, God was always ready to save his people when they repented and turned to him again. The life and achievements of David impressed the people of Israel so much that later in times of national address, when they longed for another king, itwas for one who would be a "son of David", that is, a descendant of David who would be like him. 18. Job: Chapter 1-42

Satan attacks a righteous man named Job, and Job and his friends argue about why terrible things are happening to him. Author: Nobody knows. The Book of Job is the story of a good man who suffers total disaster, losing all his children and property and suffering with a repulsive disease. Then in three series of poetic dialogues the author shows how Job's friends and Job himself react to these calamities. In the end, God himself, whose dealings with humanity have been a prominent part of the discission, appears to Job. Job's friends try to explain his suffering in traditional religious terms. Believing that in this life God always rewards good and punishes evil, Job's friends assume that the sufferings of Job can only mean that he has sinned. They offer him advice based on their assumptions. But for Job this is too simple; he knows he does not deserve such cruel punishment, because he has been an unusually good and righteous man. He cannot understand how God can let so much evil happen to someone like himself while other obviously wicked people seem to prosper. Job boldly challenges God. He does not lose his faith, but he longs to be justidied before God and the to regain his honour as a good man. God does not give the answer to Job's questions which Job might have expected. Instead God responds to Job's faith by giving him a powerful, poetic picture of his devine power and wisdom. Job then realises that God is in control and humbly acknowledges God as wise and great, repenting of the wild and angry words he had used. The prose conclusion records how Job is restored to his former condition, with even greater prosperity than before. God reprimands Job's friends for failing to understand the meaning of Job's suffering. Only Job had really sensed that God is greater than traditional religion had depicted him. 19. Psalms: Chapter 1-150

A collection of 150 songs that Israel sang to God (and to each other)—kind of like a hymnal for the ancient Israelites. Author: So many authors including David. The Book of Psalms is the hymn book and prayer book of the Bible. While some were famousy composed by David, the whole collection was the work of different authors over a long period of time. These hymns and prayers were collected and used by the people of Israel in their worship. and eventually this collection was incuded in their Scriptures. These religious poems are of many kinds: there are hymns of praise and worship of God; prayers for help. protection, and salvation; pleas for forgiveness; songs of thanksgiving for God's blessings; and petitions for the punishment of enemies. These prayers are both personal and national; some portray the most intimate feelings of one person, while others represent the needs and feelings of all the people of God. Psalm 23, the 'Shepherd Psalm, is one of the best-known and best loved passages of Scripture. The psalms were used by Jesus, quoted by the writers of the New Testament, and became the treasured book of worship of the Christian Church from its very beginning. 20. Proverbs: Chapter 1-31

A collection of sayings written to help people make wise decisions that bring about justice. Author: Solomon and other wise men. The Book of Proverbs is a collection of moral and religious teachings, mostly in the form of short sayings and proverbs. Much of it has to do with practical, everyday concerns. It begins with the reminder that "The fear of the LoRD is the beginning of knowledge" (1:7), and then goes on to deal with matters not only of religious morality, but also of common sense and good manners. Its many short sayings reveal the insights of ancient Israelite teachers about what a wise person will do in certain situations. Some of these concern family relations, others business dealings. Some deal with matters of etiquette in social relationships, and others with the need for self-control. Much is said about such qualities as humility, patience, respet for the poor, and loyalty to friends. While what was thought to be good behaviour in ancient Israelite society underlies Proverbs, much of its advice is still as relevant today as it ever was. THE GOSPELS

The New Testament includes 27 books about Jesus’ ministry and what it means to follow him. The first four books of the New Testament are called the Gospels.

40. Mathew: Chapter 1-28

This is an account of Jesus’ life, death, and resurrection, focusing on Jesus’ role as the true king of the Jews. Author: Matthew. The Gospel of Matthew tells the good news that Jesus is the promised Saviour, the one through whom God fuifilled the promises he made to his people in the Old Testament. This good news is not only for the Jewish people, among whom Jesus was born and lived, but for the whole world. Matthew is carefully arranged. It begins with the birth of Jesus, goes on to describe his baptism and temptation, and then takes up his ministry of preaching, teaching, and healing in Galleel. After this the Gospel records Jesus' journey from Galilee to Jerusalem and the events of Jesus' last week, culminating in his crucifixion and resurrection. This Gospel presenits Jesus as the great Teacher, who has the authority to intepret the Law of God, and who teaches about God's kingdom, what Matthew so frequenty records as the "kingdom of heaven". Much of Jesus' teaching is gathered by subject matter into five collections: (1) The Sermon on the Mount, which concerns the character, duties, privileges, and destiny of the citizens of the kingdom of heaven (chapters 5-7); 2) Instructions to the tweve disciples for their mission (chapter 10); G) Parables about the kingdom of heaven (chapter 13): (4) Teaching on the meaning of discipleship (chapter 18), and (5) Teaching about the end of the present age and the coming of the kingdom of heaven (chapters 24-25). 41. Mark: Chapter 1-16

This brief account of Jesus’ earthly ministry highlights Jesus’ authority and servanthood. Author: John Mark. The Gospel of Mark begins with the statement that it is "the gospel about Jesus Christ, the Son of God". Jesus is pictured as a man of action and authority. His authority is seen in his teaching, in his power over demons, and in forgiving people's sins. Jesus speaks of hinself as the Son of Man, who came to give his life to set people free from sin. Mark presernts the story of Jesus in a straightforward, vigorous way, with emphasis on what Jesus did, rather than on his words and teachings. Conscious that so many readers would not have a Jewish background, Mark contains careful explanations of Jewish culture and local geography,. After a brief prologue about John the Baptist and the baptism and temptation of Jesus, the writer immediately takes up Jesus" ministry of healing and teaching. As time goes on, the followers of Jesus come to understand him better, but Jesus' opponents become more hostile. The closing chapters report the events of Jesus' last week of earthly life, especially hs crucifixion and resurrection. The two endings to the Gospel, which are enclosed in brackets, are generally regarded as written later by someone other than the author of Mark. 42. Luke: Chapter 1-24

Luke writes the most thorough account of Jesus’ life, pulling together eyewitness testimonies to tell the full story of Jesus. Author: Luke. The Gospel of Luke presents Jesus as both the promised Saviour of Israel and as the Saviour of the whole human race. Luke records that Jesus was called by the Spirit of the Lord to "preach good news to the poor" (4:18), and this Gospel is filed with a concern for people with all kinds of need. The note of joy is also prominent in Luke, especially in the opening chapters that announce the coming of Jesus, and again at the conclusion, when Jesus ascends to heaven. The story of the growth and spread of the Christian faith after the ascension of Jesus is told by the same writer in Acts which, like Luke, is dedicated to the unknown Theophilus, the writer's friend or perhaps his sponsor. There are two sections (chapters 1-2 and 9-19) which contain much material that is found only in this Gospel, such as the stories about the song of the angels and the shepherds' visit at the birth of Jesus, Jesus in the Temple as a boy, and the parables of the Good Sarmaritan and the Lost Son. Throughout the Gospel great emphasis is placed on prayer, the Holy Spirit, the role of women in the ministry of Jesus, and God's forgiverness of sins. 45. Romans: Chapter 1-16

Paul summarizes how the gospel of Jesus works in a letter to the churches at Rome, where he plans to visit. Author: Paul. Paul wrote his important Letter to the Romans to prepare the way for a visit he planned to make to the church at Rome. His plan was to work among the Christians there for a while and then, with their support, to go on to Spain. He wrote this detailed and closely-argued letter to explain his understanding of the Christian faith and its practical implications for the lives of Christians. Romans contains Paul's most complete statenent of his message. After greeting the people of the church at Rome and teling them of his prayers for then, Paul states the theme of the letter: "For in the gospel a righteousness from God is revealed, a righteousness that is by faith from first to last" (1:17). Paul then develops this theme. The whole human race, both Jew and Gentile, needs to be put right with God, for all alike are under the power of sin. People are put right with God through faith in Jesus Christ. Next Paul describes the new life in union with Christ that results from this new relationship with God. The believer has peace with God and is set free by God's Spirit from the power of sin and death. In chapters 5-8 Paul also discusses the purpose of the Law of God and the pOwer of God's Spirit in the believer's life. Then the apostle wrestles with the question of how Jews and Gentiles fit into the plan of God for humanity. He concludes that the Jewish rejection of Jesus is part of God's plan for bringing the whole human race within the reach of God's grace in Jesus Christ, and he believes that the Jevws will not always reject Jesus. Finally Paul writes about how the Christian life should be lived, especially about the way of love in relations with others. He takes up such themes as service to God, the duty of Christians to the state and to one another, and questions of conscience. He ends the letter with personal messages and with words of praise to God.
