Galatians: Chapter 1-6



Paul hears that the Galatian churches have been lead to think that salvation comes from the law of Moses, and writes a (rather heated) letter telling them where the false teachers have it wrong. Author: Paul. The dilemma of whether one must also keep the Law of Moses in order to be a true Christian arose, especially among Christians with a Jewish background, as the good news about Jesus started to be broadcast and received among people who were not Jews. Pal had maintained that this wasn't essential and that the only solid foundation for life in Christ was faith, which is how one is reconciled to God. However, there had emerged opponents of Paul in the churches of Galatia, a Roman province in Asia, who insisted that in order for gentile (non-Jewish) Christians to be in harmony with God, they too must uphold the Jewish Law. The purpose of Paul's Letter to the Galatians was to re-establish real faith and practice among those who had been deceived by this erroneous doctrine. Paul first asserts his claim to be an apostle of Jesus Christ. He maintains that God, and not any hua authority, called him to be an apostle, and that his mission was specifically to the non-Jews. In the next chapters, he explores the claim that humans are made straight with God solely by faith and not via the obedience of the Law. Paul demonstrates how the love that springs from faith in Christ flows easily into Christian behavior.
