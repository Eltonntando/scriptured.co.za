Isaiah: Chapter 1-66



God sends the prophet Isaiah to warn Israel of future judgment—but also to tell them about a coming king and servant who will “bear the sins of many.” Author: Isaiah (and maybe some of his followers). The 6f Book A prominent prophet who lived in Jerusalem in the second part of the eighth century BC is the inspiration for the name Isaiah. Three major sections may be distinguished in this book: (1) The chapters 1–39 were written while Assyria, a strong neighbor, threatened Judah, the southern kingdom. Isaiah recognized that Judah's survival was actually threatened by its own sin, disobedience to God, and lack of faith, not just by the strength of Assyria. The prophet urged the populace and its leaders to live righteously and justly, warning that disobeying God would result in disaster and calamity if they did not. Additionally, Isaah predicted a period of universal peace and the arrival of a Davidic descendent who would become the perfect king. (2) Chapters 40 to 55 come from a period when many Judahites were crushed and without hope in exile in Babylon. The prophet declared that God will deliver his people from slavery and bring them back to Jerusalem to start over. These chapters emphasize the fact that God is the author of history and that part of his purpose for his people is to spread blessings through Israel to all peoples. Christians believe that the Old Testament's most well-known passages concerning "the Servant of the Lord" ultimately allude to Jesus. (3) The chapters 56–66 are primarily written to people who were living in Jerusalem at the time and required confidence that God would keep his promises to the people. Concern is exhibited for justice and righteousness as well as for keeping the Sabbath, offering sacrifices, and praying. A important verse is Isaiah 61:1-2, where Jesus describes his calling at the start of his mission.
